找不到工作的毕业生，真正卡住的是“学习系统”

Bloomberg Opinion 这篇 Can't Find a Job After Graduation? Blame WFH, Not AI 的标题很有意思，因为它把一个已经被 AI 叙事占满的问题重新拉回到组织本身：为什么现在的公司不太愿意招新人？

我没有直接读取完整 Bloomberg 付费正文，所以这里不是逐段复述原文，而是基于标题、Bloomberg 同主题新闻摘要、New York Fed 原始研究，以及 AP/ABC、J.P. Morgan、NACE 等公开资料做的综合解读。我的结论是：这件事不能简单归因于“AI 抢了新人饭碗”，真正卡住的是企业培养新人的学习系统。

文章大概在讲什么

Bloomberg Opinion 的表层观点大概率是：最近大学毕业生找工作困难，不能只怪 AI，远程办公可能是更直接的原因。

这个判断来自 New York Fed 2026 年 6 月 1 日发布的研究 Remote Work Leaves Younger Workers Sidelined。研究的核心说法很直接：年轻人失业率在疫情后明显上升，而远程办公也在同一时期显著普及；两者之间存在重要关系，因为远程办公让管理者更难训练和指导新员工。作者估计，远程办公可以解释年轻大学毕业生近期失业上升的 64%（New York Fed: link）。

New York Fed 的数据里有几个关键点。

年轻大学毕业生，也就是 29 岁以下大学毕业人群，失业率从 2017-2019 年平均 3.1% 上升到 2022-2025 年平均 3.7%。更有经验的大学毕业生失业率反而从 1.9% 小幅降到 1.8%。在可远程岗位中，年轻人的失业率上升接近 1 个百分点，而年长员工的失业率略降；在不可远程岗位中，这种年轻/年长差距没有同样持续扩大（New York Fed: link）。

AP/ABC 的报道也复述了这个机制：研究比较了软件工程等可远程岗位和护理等不可远程岗位，发现年轻大学毕业生在可远程岗位中的失业率上升更明显，而年长员工在同类岗位中的失业率略降（ABC/AP: link）。

研究还处理了一个很自然的反问：是不是 AI 造成的？New York Fed 的答案是，至少到目前为止，AI 不是主要解释。年轻毕业生失业率上升早于生成式 AI 的快速扩散；即使控制不同职业的 AI 暴露度，年轻和年长员工之间的差异仍然存在。

为什么远程办公会伤害新人

新人和熟手的差别，不只是技能水平，而是需要多少反馈。

一个新人刚进入公司时，真正要学的不是“怎么完成一个任务”这么简单，而是什么算好结果。哪里该多问一句，哪里可以自己决定，什么代码 review 会被打回来，什么文档让别人放心，什么沟通方式会减少协作成本。这些东西通常不是 formal training 教出来的，而是在旁边看、被打断、被纠偏、参与上下文流动中学会的。

New York Fed 的企业级数据正好支持这个机制。他们使用一家 Fortune 500 科技公司的内部数据，发现员工坐得更近时能收到更多反馈和 mentorship；距离拉开后，反馈显著减少，年轻员工受影响更大。当公司办公室关闭、员工远程工作时，公司更少招聘经验不足的人，更多招聘有经验的人；办公室重开后，公司又转向招聘年轻员工。但即使办公室重开，对于分布式团队，公司仍然更偏好有经验的人（New York Fed: link）。

这解释了一个重要悖论：远程办公对熟手和新人不是同一种福利。

对熟手来说，远程办公降低通勤，减少干扰，扩大地理选择。对新人来说，它可能减少进入真实工作系统的机会。Philadelphia Fed 对 WFH 的综述也强调，远程办公的好处并不均匀，更多流向高技能、高收入、高教育程度的人；本科及以上学历者远程办公率显著高于低学历人群，金融保险等知识工作行业远程比例最高（Philadelphia Fed: link）。

也就是说，远程办公最适合已经掌握工作方式的人；但最需要进入这些岗位的年轻人，恰恰最缺少那套隐性工作方式。

AI 不是无关，而是第二层压力

说“远程办公比 AI 更能解释近期失业上升”，不等于 AI 不重要。

J.P. Morgan 2025 年的分析给了另一面：大学毕业生失业率在 2025 年 3 月达到 5.8%，是四年多高点；AI 暴露较高的专业，如计算机工程、平面设计、工业工程、建筑等，也出现失业上升；部分科技行业，如 cloud、web search、computer systems design，在 ChatGPT 发布后就业增长趋平（J.P. Morgan: link）。J.P. Morgan 同时也承认，除部分科技行业外，目前还难以证明 AI 已经在全经济范围内显著改变就业结构。

所以更准确的图景是：AI 还未必是统计上的最大解释变量，但它已经改变了企业对初级岗位的预期。

以前新人可以从低杠杆但可训练的任务开始，比如整理资料、写初稿、做基础分析、修小 bug、跑报表。这些任务本身不是最高价值，但它们是进入真实系统的入口。AI 把这些任务的执行成本降下来后，企业会问：为什么不让一个 senior 加 AI 做掉？为什么还要多招一个需要带的人？

这就是瓶颈转移：执行变便宜后，价值从“能做事”转向“能判断、能验证、能承担上下文”。新人最缺的正是这些东西。

真正的问题：公司把 apprenticeship 外包给了运气

我觉得 Bloomberg/Fed 这条线最有价值的地方，是它把讨论从“AI 是否抢工作”拉回到一个更底层的问题：现代公司到底还会不会培养人？

很多公司嘴上说需要人才管线，但实际组织设计越来越像“即插即用的熟手市场”。远程办公让协作更依赖文档、会议、异步沟通和清晰 owner；AI 又让产出速度变快、验证成本变高。这样的环境对有经验的人非常友好，因为他们已经知道怎么拆问题、怎么判断质量、怎么向上管理不确定性。但对新人来说，这些都是还没学会的工作元技能。

如果公司没有刻意设计 onboarding、mentorship、代码/文档 review、shadowing、pairing 和短反馈循环，那么“回办公室”也不一定能解决问题。New York Fed 原文下面甚至有评论质疑：这可能只是相关性，RTO 后招聘年轻人也可能是因为熟手离职，而不一定是公司更愿意训练新人。这个质疑有道理，说明不能把办公室当万能药（New York Fed 评论区: link）。

但反过来讲，远程优先公司如果没有强反馈机制，也确实更容易把新人放在系统外面。新人看不到上下文，不知道别人怎么做判断，不知道什么算好，只能在 Slack、ticket、AI 生成答案和面试失败之间来回撞墙。

对毕业生意味着什么

对毕业生来说，这个趋势的现实含义不太舒服：只会“完成任务”越来越不够，需要更早证明自己已经部分具备熟手的工作方式。

更有用的策略不是泛泛地说“学 AI”，而是构造可验证的工作样本：能展示你如何发现问题、拆解问题、做 tradeoff、验证结果、吸收反馈。NACE 的 2026 趋势页也显示，雇主越来越强调 skills-based hiring；70% 的受访雇主使用技能型招聘，高于前一年的 65%；同时 NACE 也列出 2026 届招聘预计增长 5.6%，但此前也有“招聘趋平”的不确定信号（NACE: link）。

这意味着市场不是完全没机会，而是机会更偏向能让雇主降低训练不确定性的人。实习、项目、开源贡献、可解释的 portfolio、清楚写出自己如何使用 AI 但又如何验证 AI 的过程，都会比“我会某技术栈”更有价值。

对公司意味着什么

公司如果真想要长期人才供给，就不能只在 RTO 和 remote 之间二选一。真正该问的是：我们的组织有没有新人学习系统？

一个更好的设计可能是混合式的：新人前几个月更高密度地 co-locate 或 pair，固定 mentor，保留 shadowing 和 review；熟手可以更灵活远程；关键工作流通过文档、录屏、代码 review、设计 review、事后复盘沉淀下来。这样 remote 不再是“把人散开”，而是把隐性知识显性化。

这也和 AI 工作流类似。AI 能降低产出成本，但如果没有验证、反馈、上下文沉淀，生成越快，错误也扩散越快。新人培养也是一样：没有反馈回路，组织就只是把新人丢进任务队列里，然后抱怨他们“不 ready”。

结论

Bloomberg 这篇文章的表层观点是：毕业生找不到工作，不要只怪 AI，远程办公可能才是更大的短期原因。

更深一层看，问题不是 remote 本身，也不是 AI 本身，而是企业在远程化和自动化之后，没有重建 apprenticeship。过去靠办公室偶遇、旁听、顺手纠错维持的学习系统，被疫情和工具革命打碎了；但很多公司只是享受了远程和 AI 的效率收益，没有为新人重建反馈结构。

所以，年轻毕业生就业困难不是单一技术冲击，而是组织学习成本重新定价的结果。熟手更值钱，新人更难进入系统。这会造成长期后果：如果一代新人缺少入口，几年后市场上就不会凭空长出足够多的熟手。

真正该担心的，不是 AI 现在已经替代了多少 entry-level job，而是企业正在失去把 entry-level 变成 mid-level 的能力。

Why New Graduates Are Stuck: The Broken Learning System

The Bloomberg Opinion piece Can't Find a Job After Graduation? Blame WFH, Not AI is interesting because it pushes against the dominant AI narrative. Instead of asking only whether AI is taking entry-level jobs, it points back to the organization: why are companies less willing to hire beginners in the first place?

I did not directly read the full Bloomberg paywalled article, so this is not a paragraph-by-paragraph summary. It is a synthesis based on the headline, Bloomberg's related news summary, the original New York Fed research, and public reporting and analysis from AP/ABC, J.P. Morgan, and NACE. My conclusion is that the problem is not simply that AI is stealing the first rung of the career ladder. The deeper problem is that companies have weakened the learning system that used to turn beginners into productive workers.

What The Article Is Probably Arguing

The surface argument of the Bloomberg Opinion piece is likely this: recent college graduates are struggling to find jobs, but AI is not the only, or even the best, explanation. Remote work may be the more immediate culprit.

That argument comes from the New York Fed's June 1, 2026 research post, Remote Work Leaves Younger Workers Sidelined. The core claim is direct: youth unemployment has risen since the pandemic, remote work has also become much more common, and the two trends are related because remote work makes it harder for managers to train and mentor new employees. The authors estimate that remote work can explain 64% of the recent increase in unemployment among young college graduates (New York Fed: link).

The New York Fed data has several important details.

Among college graduates under 29, unemployment rose from an average of 3.1% in 2017-2019 to 3.7% in 2022-2025. For more experienced college graduates, unemployment actually dipped from 1.9% to 1.8%. In remotable occupations, young workers' unemployment rose by almost one percentage point, while older workers' unemployment marginally declined. In non-remotable occupations, the same persistent young-versus-old gap did not appear (New York Fed: link).

AP/ABC described the same mechanism: the study compares remotable jobs such as software development with non-remotable jobs such as nursing, and finds that young college graduates were hit harder in remotable roles while older workers in those roles did slightly better (ABC/AP: link).

The study also addresses the obvious AI question. The New York Fed's answer is that, at least so far, AI is not the main explanation. The rise in unemployment among young graduates began before generative AI diffused widely, and the young-versus-old gap persists even after controlling for occupational exposure to AI.

Why Remote Work Hurts Beginners

The difference between a beginner and an experienced worker is not just skill level. It is how much feedback they need.

When a new graduate joins a company, the real learning is not only about completing tasks. It is about learning what counts as good work. When should you ask one more question? When can you decide on your own? What kind of code review gets rejected? What kind of document makes stakeholders trust you? What communication style reduces coordination cost? These things are rarely learned through formal training alone. They are learned by watching, being interrupted, getting corrected, and participating in the flow of context.

The New York Fed's firm-level evidence supports this mechanism. Using proprietary data from a Fortune 500 technology company, the researchers find that employees receive more feedback and mentorship when they sit near colleagues. When distance increases, feedback drops sharply, and younger workers are more affected. When the company's offices were closed, it hired fewer inexperienced workers and more experienced workers. After offices reopened, it shifted back toward hiring younger workers. But for distributed teams, it continued to prefer more experienced workers (New York Fed: link).

This explains an important paradox: remote work is not the same benefit for experienced workers and beginners.

For experienced workers, remote work reduces commuting, cuts distractions, and expands geographic choice. For beginners, it can reduce access to the real work system. A Philadelphia Fed review of working from home also emphasizes that WFH benefits are unevenly distributed and flow more heavily to high-skill, high-income, highly educated workers. Workers with a bachelor's degree or above have much higher telework rates than less-educated workers, and knowledge-work industries such as finance and insurance have especially high telework rates (Philadelphia Fed: link).

In other words, remote work works best for people who already know how work works. The people most trying to enter those jobs are precisely the ones who have not yet learned the hidden operating system.

AI Is Not Irrelevant. It Is The Second Layer Of Pressure

Saying that remote work explains more of the recent unemployment increase than AI does not mean AI is irrelevant.

J.P. Morgan's 2025 analysis gives the other side of the picture. The unemployment rate among college graduates reached 5.8% in March 2025, the highest in more than four years. AI-exposed majors such as computer engineering, graphic design, industrial engineering, and architecture saw rising unemployment. Some technology industries, including cloud, web search, and computer systems design, stopped growing around the end of 2022, shortly after ChatGPT was released (J.P. Morgan: link). J.P. Morgan also notes that outside selected tech industries, it remains hard to prove that AI has already reshaped employment across the broader economy.

So the more accurate picture is this: AI may not yet be the largest statistical driver, but it has already changed how companies think about entry-level work.

In the past, beginners could start with low-leverage but trainable tasks: gathering information, writing first drafts, doing basic analysis, fixing small bugs, running reports. These tasks were not the highest-value work, but they were the gateway into the real system. Once AI lowers the cost of those tasks, companies naturally ask: why not have a senior person plus AI do it instead? Why hire another person who needs to be trained?

This is a bottleneck shift. When execution gets cheaper, the bottleneck moves to judgment, verification, and context ownership. Those are exactly the things beginners have not yet acquired.

The Real Problem: Companies Have Outsourced Apprenticeship To Luck

The most useful part of the Bloomberg/Fed argument is that it moves the debate away from the simplistic question of whether AI is taking jobs and back toward a more basic question: do modern companies still know how to train people?

Many companies say they need a talent pipeline, but their operating model increasingly looks like a market for plug-and-play experienced workers. Remote work makes collaboration depend more on documents, meetings, asynchronous updates, and clear ownership. AI makes output faster and verification more important. This environment is friendly to experienced workers because they already know how to break down problems, judge quality, and manage uncertainty upward. For beginners, those are precisely the meta-skills they have not yet learned.

If a company does not deliberately design onboarding, mentorship, code and document review, shadowing, pairing, and short feedback loops, then returning to the office will not magically solve the problem. A comment under the New York Fed post makes a fair critique: the pattern may be partly correlational, and post-RTO hiring of young workers could reflect experienced workers leaving rather than companies becoming more willing to train beginners (New York Fed comments: link). That critique matters. The office is not a cure-all.

But the opposite is also true. Remote-first companies without strong feedback systems can easily leave beginners outside the system. New hires cannot see the context, cannot observe how others make decisions, and cannot tell what good looks like. They end up bouncing between Slack, tickets, AI-generated answers, and failed interviews.

What This Means For Graduates

For graduates, the uncomfortable implication is that merely being able to complete tasks is becoming less valuable. You need to show, earlier than before, that you have some of the working style of an experienced employee.

The better strategy is not simply to say “learn AI.” It is to build verifiable work samples: examples that show how you identify problems, break them down, make tradeoffs, verify results, and absorb feedback. NACE's 2026 trend page shows that employers are increasingly using skills-based hiring; 70% of surveyed employers report using it, up from 65% the previous year. NACE also lists a projected 5.6% increase in hiring for the Class of 2026, alongside earlier signs of flat hiring and uncertainty (NACE: link).

That means the market is not closed. But opportunities increasingly favor candidates who reduce the employer's training uncertainty. Internships, projects, open-source contributions, explainable portfolios, and clear examples of how you use AI and verify AI outputs matter more than simply listing a technology stack.

What This Means For Companies

If companies actually want a long-term talent supply, they should not frame the issue as a binary choice between RTO and remote. The better question is: does the organization have a learning system for beginners?

A better design may be hybrid. New hires spend the first few months with denser co-location or pairing. They get fixed mentors, structured shadowing, and frequent review. Experienced workers retain more flexibility. Key workflows are captured through documents, screen recordings, code reviews, design reviews, and postmortems. In that model, remote work no longer means scattering people. It means making tacit knowledge explicit.

This is similar to AI workflows. AI can reduce the cost of output, but without verification, feedback, and context, faster generation just spreads mistakes faster. Training beginners works the same way. Without feedback loops, the organization is just throwing new hires into the task queue and then complaining that they are not ready.

Conclusion

The surface point of the Bloomberg piece is that graduates struggling to find work should not blame only AI. Remote work may be the larger short-term cause.

The deeper point is that the problem is not remote work itself, nor AI itself. The problem is that companies moved into remote and automated work without rebuilding apprenticeship. The old learning system, based on office proximity, overheard context, and casual correction, was broken by the pandemic and tool shift. Many companies enjoyed the efficiency gains of remote work and AI without rebuilding the feedback structure that turns beginners into productive workers.

So the labor-market problem facing young graduates is not a single technology shock. It is a repricing of organizational learning cost. Experienced workers become more valuable. Beginners become harder to absorb. That has long-run consequences: if one generation cannot enter the system, the market will not magically produce enough experienced workers a few years later.

The real concern is not how many entry-level jobs AI has already replaced. It is whether companies are losing the ability to turn entry-level workers into mid-level workers.
