日本 naphtha 冲击后续推演：从黑白零食包装到跨行业材料瓶颈

日期：2026-05-19
对象：日本 naphtha / 石化衍生材料供应冲击、食品包装黑白化、食品外行业扩散
问题：Calbee 黑白包装之后，后续可能发生什么？类似冲击是否会出现在食品外的其他领域？
性质：深度调研报告；公开资料研究记录；不构成投资建议

核心结论

Calbee 黑白包装不是一个“薯片包装新闻”，而是日本下游制造体系正在暴露的一个材料瓶颈信号。更准确的框架不是“伊朗战争导致油墨短缺”，而是：naphtha 作为石化上游 feedstock，正在通过树脂、薄膜、油墨、溶剂、胶粘剂、涂层、合成橡胶和包装加工产能，传导到食品、日用品、建材、物流、医卫、汽车和电子等行业。

这次冲击最值得关注的地方，是政府口径和企业行为之间的落差。政府可以说“全国总量可以确保”，但企业实际面对的是某一种白墨、某一种食品级薄膜、某一种胶粘剂、某个包装印刷档期、某个容器模具、某个交货窗口是否可用。宏观总量够，不等于下游规格、认证、交期和转换产能够。

食品行业最先可见，是因为食品包装既消耗巨大，又直接绑定出货。薯片、番茄酱、纳豆、乳酸菌饮料、意面胶带等只是早期案例。后续更大的方向是：价格上涨在夏秋集中出现，SKU 被清洗，包装从“营销资产”退化为“受限产能”，限定设计和小批量产品减少。

食品外也会发生类似事情，但表现形式不同：建材/卫浴会表现为订单暂停或交期延长；日用品会表现为纸尿裤、卫生用品、纸品、垃圾袋涨价或限购；物流会表现为拉伸膜、OPP 胶带、PP 打包带涨价；涂料/印刷/汽车维修会表现为溶剂和稀释剂短缺；医卫材料的风险更偏基础设施连续性，而不是包装外观。

研究方法和源发现

本轮按 workspace 的 deep research survey 流程重做，先用 Tavily 做 3 组 broad source discovery：

  • Japan naphtha shortage packaging supply chain food non-food industries 2026
  • 日本 ナフサ不足 包装 食品以外 影響 業界 2026
  • ナフサ不足 食品包装以外 トイレ バス 建材 医療 日用品 2026

随后补充抓取和交叉验证了帝国数据银行、时事通信、NHK WORLD、日清食品集团、Nippon TV、Mainichi、Inbound Logistics 等来源。报告中把媒体报道、公司公告、行业调查和推演分开标注。

关键事实底座

1. 企业影响已经不是个案

生団連针对会员企业的调查显示，回答的 102 家企业中，44.1% 已经受到 naphtha 供给不安影响；若现状持续，31.4% 预计 3 个月内会受影响，14.7% 预计 3 个月到 1 年内会受影响。应对措施中，72.5% 提到产品涨价，47.1% 提到部分产品供给限制，42.2% 提到内容量或规格调整，35.3% 提到终卖或休卖可能，57.8% 表示替代材料调达量不足或没有替代品（Jiji: https://www.jiji.com/jc/article?g=eco&k=2026042700686）。

这组数字比 Calbee 本身重要。它说明黑白包装不是单家公司新闻，而是一个已经扩散到食品、零售和消费品链条的下游经营问题。

2. 制造业暴露面很广

帝国数据银行对 52 家主要石油化学产品制造商的直接和间接交易链做分析，称与 naphtha 相关产品存在采购风险的制造业约 46,741 家，占其可统计制造业样本约 3 成。它特别指出 naphtha 是乙烯、丙烯等基础化学品和合成树脂等中间材料的上游，供给限制或高价会压迫中小制造业，并通过产品价格影响生活（TDB: https://www.tdb.co.jp/report/industry/20260417-naphtha26fy/）。

这个口径意味着：食品包装只是最早被消费者看到的一块，真正暴露面在制造业中间层。

3. 食品价格冲击有滞后性

帝国数据银行 2026 年 5 月食品主要 195 社价格改定调查显示，5 月食品涨价只有 70 个品目，表面上不严重；但“包装・资材”已经在涨价理由中达到 69.9%，为 2023 年以来最高节奏之一。TDB 判断，naphtha 供给难若持续，食品包装薄膜和标签油墨等中间材料涨价会滞后显现，早则今夏、迟则秋季，食品涨价潮可能再燃（TDB: https://www.tdb.co.jp/report/economic/20260430-neage26y05/）。

这说明后续不是立即全面断货，而是先由包材/中间材涨价，再经过合同、库存和零售谈判，延迟传导到终端价格。

食品行业：后续更可能发生什么

方向 A：低油墨包装从个案变成菜单式选择

Calbee 的黑白包装是最醒目的版本，但 Kagome 采用的是另一条路：减少番茄插图、扩大透明区域，从而降低油墨使用；NHK WORLD 报道称新包装会从 5 月下旬左右上架，直到油墨供应稳定（NHK WORLD: https://www3.nhk.or.jp/nhkworld/en/news/20260515_B1/）。

Nisshin Seifun Welna 则不是改主包装，而是停止在意面捆扎胶带上印煮制时间（NHK WORLD 同上）。这代表企业会按“重要性排序”削减印刷：先砍辅助信息、装饰图案、二级包装、胶带和外袋，再动主包装。

后续可预期的低油墨做法包括：

  • 黑白/双色包装；
  • 扩大透明窗口；
  • 减少食品图片、角色、底色和渐变；
  • 取消胶带、外袋、束带上的非必要印刷；
  • 停止季节限定/区域限定包装；
  • 使用通用包装加贴最少量标签。

方向 B：SKU 清洗会比货架黑白化更重要

食品企业不会平均分配稀缺包材，而会优先保高销量、高毛利、战略主力 SKU。最容易被压缩的是：

  • 限定口味和季节款；
  • 地方限定包装；
  • 低毛利但包装复杂的产品；
  • 小批量促销装；
  • 新品首发；
  • 多规格小包装。

这类变化不一定会被媒体命名为“naphtha shortage”，但会表现为新品节奏变慢、限定款减少、同一品牌的规格趋同。

方向 C：涨价、缩量和休卖会同时出现

Mizkan 已经因为包装材料成本上升，对部分纳豆商品 6 月起涨价 6%-20%，并暂停 4 个纳豆产品销售（NHK WORLD backstory: https://www3.nhk.or.jp/nhkworld/en/news/backstories/4799/）。日清食品集团旗下日清ヨーク宣布，乳酸菌饮料部分产品自 2026 年 7 月 1 日纳品分起上调出货价格 3%-8%，理由是国际情势影响导致包装资材价格高涨，超过自助努力吸收范围（Nissin: https://www.nissin.com/jp/news/13755/）。

后续食品行业会出现三种并行路径：

  • 明涨价：直接提高出货价或建议零售价；
  • 隐性涨价：减少内容量、减少附赠、调整规格；
  • 断尾求生：暂停低销量 SKU，把包材资源集中给主力。

食品外行业：哪些领域会出现类似事情

1. 建材与卫浴：从包装问题变成订单问题

TOTO 是食品外最清晰的案例之一。Nippon TV 和 Mainichi 报道称，TOTO 2026 年 4 月暂停组合浴室/预制浴室新订单，原因是 naphtha 短缺影响了将薄膜贴到浴室墙面和天花板的胶粘剂，以及部分人工大理石浴缸的涂层材料。TOTO 表示马桶等主力产品暂未受影响，但订单恢复时间不明（Nippon TV: https://www.ntv.co.jp/englishnews/articles/2021z0we6iqm15ez9a07.html, Mainichi: https://mainichi.jp/english/articles/20260414/p2g/00m/0bu/004000c）。Mainichi 另有报道显示，石化材料采购困难已经让日本部分建筑和大规模住宅维修工程暂停；埼玉一处公寓维修因防水片等相关材料短缺被迫中断（Mainichi: https://mainichi.jp/english/articles/20260424/p2a/00m/0bu/016000c）。

这和食品包装不同。食品体现为包装降级、涨价、SKU 调整；卫浴建材体现为“装配系统缺一个胶/涂层就无法交付”。后续类似风险行业包括：

  • 住宅设备：浴室、厨房、洗面台、地板、壁材；
  • 建材：PVC 管、保温材料、胶粘剂、密封材料、涂料；
  • 装修施工：因溶剂/胶/涂料价格波动导致报价有效期缩短。

2. 物流包材：看不见但几乎所有行业都用

Inbound Logistics 把 naphtha 冲击称为“the plastic wrapped around everything they move”的风险。文章指出，原油上涨推高 naphtha，再传到 ethylene、propylene、PE、PP、PET、PVC resin，最终进入包装部门；托盘固定用 stretch film 等物流包材价格已显著上升，企业开始寻找更高效缠绕方式（Inbound Logistics: https://www.inboundlogistics.com/articles/the-invisible-shortage-how-petrochemical-shortages-could-impact-packaging）。

物流包材是典型二阶传导：它不是最终商品，却进入所有商品的运输成本。后续可能表现为：

  • stretch film、PP strapping、OPP tape、气泡膜、缓冲材涨价；
  • 仓库改变打包规范，减少缠绕层数；
  • 物流破损率和保险/理赔风险上升；
  • 小电商和中小制造商更难吸收成本。

3. 日用品、纸品、卫生用品：不是“纸”本身，而是树脂、包材、油墨和燃料

NHK WORLD backstory 报道，Daio Paper 计划对 8 月交付的家庭用和商用产品涨价 15% 以上，涉及 tissue、toilet paper、kitchen paper、diapers、sanitary products，理由包括燃料、物流、包装材料和油墨成本上升（NHK WORLD: https://www3.nhk.or.jp/nhkworld/en/news/backstories/4799/）。同一篇还提到日本政府提醒公众不要恐慌购买垃圾袋，因为垃圾袋也含 naphtha 衍生材料。

这类行业最可能出现的不是“黑白包装”，而是：

  • 纸巾、厕纸、厨房纸、纸尿裤、卫生用品涨价；
  • 外包装薄膜变薄或规格调整；
  • 大包装/补充装相对更受推；
  • 垃圾袋等低价刚需品出现囤货和限购舆情。

4. 涂料、印刷、溶剂：价格波动和受注限制风险更高

食品包装新闻里的“油墨”只是更大油墨/溶剂体系的一角。naphtha 衍生物会进入溶剂、树脂、涂料、稀释剂、胶粘剂、印刷油墨。Tavily 源发现和多家行业报道都指向：涂料用 thinner、油墨 resin/solvent、printing converter 的交期和成本会成为瓶颈。

这个领域的冲击形式包括：

  • 印刷厂受注限制或交期延长；
  • 价格报价有效期缩短；
  • 食品包材外，商业印刷、标签、工业标识也涨价；
  • 汽修、建筑涂装、制造涂装现场因溶剂价格和供应不稳而推迟施工。

5. 汽车、电子、机械：不是整车缺 naphtha，而是小件断供风险

TDB 的供应链分析强调，naphtha 衍生产品经基础化学品、合成树脂等进入电气产品、汽车部件、衣料、医药等广泛最终产品（TDB: https://www.tdb.co.jp/report/industry/20260417-naphtha26fy/）。

汽车和电子行业的风险通常不是消费者看到包装变色，而是：

  • 线束包覆、连接器、密封件、内饰树脂件、橡胶件成本上升；
  • 某个低价值小部件断供拖慢整机/整车组装；
  • 供应商要求调价，整车厂/电子厂向下游压价或重配供应商；
  • 替代材料需要认证周期，无法立即切换。

这个逻辑类似疫情期间汽车缺芯：不是所有芯片都贵，而是少数关键件让整条线停摆。naphtha 冲击下，类似风险会出现在胶、密封、树脂、薄膜、绝缘和包装件上。

6. 医卫材料：优先级最高，但替代最难

医卫材料大量使用 PP、PE、PVC、EVA、合成橡胶和包装灭菌材料。一次性手套、注射器、输液袋、导管、透析回路、药品泡罩、无菌包装都可能涉及 naphtha 衍生材料。

医卫领域已经出现更明确的预警。Reuters 报道称，如果 naphtha 供应不足继续恶化，透析和手术相关的高紧急性医疗器械中，部分品目可能在 4 月中旬到 8 月左右出现出货困难；高市首相也被认为已掌握相关风险，后续政府应对成为焦点（Reuters: https://jp.reuters.com/markets/commodities/HJNOEZOE4RPQXNDYBVOA3CXZMA-2026-03-27/）。机制上，医卫材料具备三个高风险特征：

  • 使用量大且一次性；
  • 食品/医疗接触材料有认证要求，替代慢；
  • 不能简单通过“少印刷”解决，因为核心是材料本体和无菌包装。

因此，医卫更可能被政府优先保障，而不是任其价格自由传导。后续若出现政策“优先配分”或医院耗材调价通知，是需要重点跟踪的信号。

为什么“总量充足”仍会出现企业降级和停单

这次研究中最关键的解释是：上游总量、下游可用性和消费端体验是三件事。

政府关心的是 naphtha 或 naphtha 衍生化学品整体能不能支撑国民经济；企业关心的是某个材料能不能在某个日期按某个规格交付。中间有多层瓶颈：

  1. feedstock：naphtha、原油、替代来源是否到港；
  2. cracker：乙烯/丙烯等基础化学品产能是否正常；
  3. resin/solvent：PE、PP、PVC、PET、溶剂、树脂是否可用；
  4. converter：薄膜、容器、胶粘剂、油墨、涂层加工是否排得上产能；
  5. compliance：食品接触、医卫、建材认证是否允许替代；
  6. user：食品厂、日用品厂、建材厂是否能在既有产线使用新材料。

只要第 3-6 层任何一层堵住，企业就会采取包装降级、涨价、限供或停单，即使宏观上“总量够”。

政策响应：从宏观保供走向下游 triage

政策层面的变化也验证了这一判断。农林水产省已设立“中东情势相关对策”门户，并围绕石油由来食品容器、包装资材、农业用塑料、润滑油等稳定供应发布信息和请求（MAFF: https://www.maff.go.jp/chuto_josei.html）。朝日新闻报道也称，农水省正在调查食品托盘、便当容器、鸡蛋盒、蔬菜包装膜、农用资材、渔具等 57 个项目的流通实态；其中米袋、发泡聚苯乙烯、农用地膜等 6 个品目被说明“已有确保目标”（Asahi: https://www.asahi.com/articles/ASV4X21WPV4XUTIL00MM.html）。

这代表政府反应正在从“naphtha 总量能否维持”转向“哪些下游生活必需品和农业/食品资材需要优先疏通”。后续若医疗耗材、食品容器、垃圾袋、农膜、建筑维修材料之间出现竞争，政策很可能采用事实上的优先序：医疗和生活必需品高于促销包装、装饰性印刷和低优先级消费品。

市场结构上，这会造成三类分化：

  • 大企业更容易拿到材料和 converter 产能，中小企业更容易被挤出；
  • 标准化包装和少 SKU 公司更有韧性，依赖小批量多设计的公司更脆弱；
  • 上游包材、油墨、薄膜、胶粘剂供应商的议价权短期上升，但也会被政府要求配合生活必需品保供。

后续情景推演

基准情景：夏秋涨价 + 包装简化常态化

概率最高。naphtha 进口和替代调达逐步改善，但下游库存、合同和加工产能有滞后。食品和日用品价格在夏秋继续上调；包装简化持续数月；企业保主力 SKU，减少非必要设计。

领先指标：

  • 7 月以后食品、日用品调价公告增加；
  • 包装资材、油墨、薄膜公司继续提价；
  • 更多包装出现“资源节约”“简易包装”说明；
  • 限定款和促销包装减少。

压力情景：下游规格瓶颈导致休卖扩散

如果中东局势持续、替代进口到货慢、converter 产能不足，冲击会从价格传导变成供应传导。部分中小食品厂、日用品厂、建材商会出现休卖、订单暂停、交期延长。

领先指标：

  • 更多公司公告“休売”“受注停止”“出荷制限”；
  • 零售端出现垃圾袋、纸尿裤、包装食品限购；
  • 包材供应商拒接新单；
  • 政府开始公开协调生活必需品包材优先供应。

转型情景：低油墨、透明、通用包装长期化

如果消费者接受简化包装，企业会把危机措施包装成环保/资源节约，并保留部分设计。长期看，包装会向标准化、少色、少图案、通用底版方向迁移。

领先指标：

  • 企业把“临时包装”改称“环保/节约包装”；
  • 新品直接采用低印刷设计；
  • 零售商接受更多通用包装和贴标方案；
  • 包装设计 KPI 从货架冲击力转向材料韧性和供应稳定性。

反转情景：上游恢复，但价格留在高位

即便霍尔木兹运输改善，企业也可能借机重订合同、提高安全库存、把过去难以转嫁的包装和物流成本纳入价格。消费者看到的可能不是继续黑白化，而是价格不回落。

领先指标：

  • 包装恢复彩色但价格不降；
  • 企业财报提到“资材成本结构性上升”；
  • 包材企业利润率改善；
  • 零售 PB 和大包装加速增长。

对不同参与者的影响

大食品品牌

有较强采购能力，能优先拿到包材，但会牺牲新品和限定款。最可能采取“主力 SKU 保供 + 低油墨包装 + 分批涨价”。

中小食品厂

风险最高。它们没有足够议价能力，也难快速承担包材涨价。若某个容器或薄膜断供，可能直接休卖。

包装/油墨/薄膜供应商

短期议价权上升，但也面临原料采购压力。能提供替代材料、低油墨方案、快速改版和认证支持的供应商会受益。

零售商和便利店

短期会被缺货和涨价夹击；中期可能提高对稳定供应商的货架权重。PB 是否受益取决于其包材供应链是否更标准化。

消费者

会先看到包装变丑、变简单，然后看到涨价、规格变小、低价商品减少。纳豆、纸品、垃圾袋、乳酸菌饮料、面包、加工食品等生活品类更容易有感。

结论：这是一场“材料基础设施”的压力测试

Calbee 黑白包装之所以重要，不是因为薯片包装变丑，而是因为它把现代消费品背后的材料基础设施暴露出来。很多产品的瓶颈并不在“能不能生产内容物”，而在能不能拿到合规包装、胶粘剂、油墨、薄膜、涂层、容器和运输包材。

所以后续判断要从三个问题入手：

  1. 有多少企业从“涨价”走向“限供/休卖”？
  2. 有多少临时简化包装被长期保留下来？
  3. 食品外是否出现更多 TOTO 式“缺一个材料就停接订单”的案例？

如果答案持续增加，这次就不是日本零食行业的趣闻，而是 naphtha 作为石化基础设施在消费端的一次系统性显影。

Japan's Naphtha Shock: From Black-and-White Snack Packages to Cross-Sector Materials Bottlenecks

Date: 2026-05-19
Subject: Japan's naphtha / petrochemical-derived materials shock, food packaging simplification, and spillovers beyond food
Question: After Calbee's black-and-white packaging move, what could happen next? Could similar effects appear outside the food industry?
Type: Deep research report; public-source research note; not investment advice

Core Conclusions

Calbee's black-and-white packaging is not merely a "potato chip package" story. It is a visible signal that Japan's downstream manufacturing system is exposing a materials bottleneck. The more accurate frame is not simply "the Iran war caused an ink shortage." It is this: naphtha, as an upstream petrochemical feedstock, is transmitting stress through resins, films, inks, solvents, adhesives, coatings, synthetic rubber, and packaging conversion capacity into food, daily necessities, construction materials, logistics, healthcare, automobiles, and electronics.

The most important part of this shock is the gap between government-level messaging and firm-level behavior. The government can say that aggregate national supply is secured, while companies still face a different reality: whether a specific white ink, food-grade film, adhesive, printing slot, container mold, or delivery window is actually available. Macro-level volume sufficiency does not equal downstream sufficiency in specifications, certification, lead times, and conversion capacity.

The food industry is the first place where this becomes visible because food packaging is both high-volume and directly tied to shipment. Potato chips, ketchup, natto, lactic acid beverages, and pasta bundling tape are early cases. The larger follow-on direction is likely to be clustered summer/autumn price increases, SKU pruning, packaging shifting from a "marketing asset" into a "constrained production input," and fewer limited-edition designs or small-batch products.

Similar effects are also likely outside food, but the symptoms will differ. Construction materials and bathroom fixtures will show up as suspended orders or longer lead times. Daily necessities will show up as higher prices or purchase limits for diapers, sanitary products, paper goods, and garbage bags. Logistics will show up as higher prices for stretch film, OPP tape, and PP strapping. Paint, printing, and automotive repair will show up as shortages of solvents and thinners. Healthcare materials are less about package appearance and more about infrastructure continuity.

Factual Base

1. The impact is no longer isolated

A Seidanren survey of member companies found that, among 102 responding firms, 44.1% had already been affected by naphtha supply uncertainty. If current conditions persist, 31.4% expect an impact within three months and 14.7% expect an impact within three months to one year. As potential responses, 72.5% cited product price increases, 47.1% cited supply restrictions for some products, 42.2% cited revisions to volume or specifications, 35.3% cited possible discontinuation or temporary suspension, and 57.8% said alternative materials were insufficient or unavailable (Jiji: https://www.jiji.com/jc/article?g=eco&k=2026042700686).

Those numbers matter more than Calbee's individual case. They show that black-and-white packaging is not a single-company story. It is a downstream operating problem already spreading across food, retail, and consumer goods.

2. Manufacturing exposure is broad

Teikoku Databank analyzed the direct and indirect transaction networks of 52 major petrochemical product manufacturers. It found that roughly 46,741 manufacturing firms may face procurement risk related to naphtha-linked products, about 30% of its countable manufacturing sample. TDB emphasizes that naphtha sits upstream of basic chemicals such as ethylene and propylene, and of intermediate materials such as synthetic resins; restrictions or high prices can pressure small and midsize manufacturers and eventually affect daily life through product prices (TDB: https://www.tdb.co.jp/report/industry/20260417-naphtha26fy/).

This means food packaging is only the most visible early piece. The real exposure sits in the manufacturing middle layer.

3. Food price impact is lagged

TDB's May 2026 price revision survey of 195 major food companies showed only 70 food price increases in May, which looks mild on the surface. But "packaging/materials" already accounted for 69.9% of cited reasons for price increases, one of the highest paces since 2023. TDB expects that if naphtha supply tightness continues, higher costs for food packaging film and label ink will emerge with a lag, potentially reigniting a broader food price-hike wave as early as summer or by autumn (TDB: https://www.tdb.co.jp/report/economic/20260430-neage26y05/).

The implication is that the next phase is not immediate across-the-board shortages. It begins with price hikes in packaging and intermediate materials, then passes through contracts, inventories, and retail negotiations before reaching consumer prices.

Food Industry: What Is More Likely Next

Direction A: Low-ink packaging becomes a menu of options, not a one-off

Calbee's black-and-white packaging is the most visible version. Kagome is taking a different route: reducing tomato illustrations and enlarging the transparent area to reduce ink use. NHK WORLD reported that the new packaging will appear from around late May and stay in place until ink supply stabilizes (NHK WORLD: https://www3.nhk.or.jp/nhkworld/en/news/20260515_B1/).

Nisshin Seifun Welna is not changing a main package. Instead, it will stop printing cooking time on the tape used to bundle spaghetti (same NHK WORLD report). This suggests companies will cut printing in order of importance: auxiliary information, decorative graphics, secondary packaging, tape, outer bags, and then main packaging.

Likely low-ink tactics include:

  • black-and-white or two-color packaging;
  • larger transparent windows;
  • fewer food photos, characters, background colors, and gradients;
  • removing nonessential printing from tape, outer bags, and bundling bands;
  • pausing seasonal or regional package designs;
  • using generic packaging plus minimal stickers.

Direction B: SKU pruning matters more than black-and-white shelves

Food companies will not distribute scarce packaging materials evenly. They will prioritize high-volume, high-margin, strategic core SKUs. The most vulnerable products are:

  • limited-edition flavors and seasonal products;
  • region-specific packaging;
  • low-margin products with complex packaging;
  • small-run promotional packs;
  • new product launches;
  • multiple small-size formats.

These changes may not be explicitly labeled as "naphtha shortage" effects. They may instead show up as slower new-product cadence, fewer limited editions, and more standardized product formats within the same brand.

Direction C: Price hikes, shrinkflation, and temporary suspensions happen together

Mizkan has already moved to raise prices by 6%-20% on some natto products from June because of higher packaging material costs, while suspending four natto products (NHK WORLD backstory: https://www3.nhk.or.jp/nhkworld/en/news/backstories/4799/). Nissin York, part of the Nissin Foods Group, announced that some lactic acid beverage products will see shipment prices rise by 3%-8% from July 1, 2026, citing packaging material cost increases driven by the international situation and costs exceeding what internal efforts can absorb (Nissin: https://www.nissin.com/jp/news/13755/).

Food companies are likely to use three paths in parallel:

  • explicit price increases: higher shipment prices or suggested retail prices;
  • implicit price increases: smaller volumes, fewer extras, or specification changes;
  • cutting the tail: suspending low-volume SKUs and concentrating packaging inputs on core products.

Beyond Food: Where Similar Effects Could Appear

1. Construction materials and bathroom fixtures: from packaging problems to order problems

TOTO is one of the clearest non-food cases. Nippon TV and Mainichi reported that in April 2026, TOTO suspended new orders for prefabricated bathroom units because the naphtha shortage affected adhesives used to attach film to bathroom walls and ceilings, as well as coating materials for some artificial marble bathtubs. TOTO said flagship products such as toilets were not yet affected, but the timing for order resumption was unclear (Nippon TV: https://www.ntv.co.jp/englishnews/articles/2021z0we6iqm15ez9a07.html, Mainichi: https://mainichi.jp/english/articles/20260414/p2g/00m/0bu/004000c). Mainichi also reported that procurement difficulties for petrochemical materials have already halted some construction and large-scale building repair work in Japan; one condominium repair project in Saitama was interrupted due to shortages of materials such as waterproof sheets (Mainichi: https://mainichi.jp/english/articles/20260424/p2a/00m/0bu/016000c).

This differs from food packaging. In food, the symptoms are package downgrades, price increases, and SKU changes. In bathroom fixtures and construction materials, one missing adhesive or coating can stop a whole assembled product or construction process. Similar risk areas include:

  • housing equipment: bathrooms, kitchens, vanities, flooring, wall materials;
  • construction materials: PVC pipes, insulation, adhesives, sealants, coatings;
  • renovation work: shorter quote validity periods because solvent, adhesive, and coating prices are volatile.

2. Logistics packaging: invisible, but used by almost every industry

Inbound Logistics frames the naphtha shock as a risk to "the plastic wrapped around everything they move." It explains that higher crude oil raises naphtha prices, which then transmit to ethylene, propylene, PE, PP, PET, and PVC resins before reaching the packaging sector. It also notes that prices for pallet-securing stretch film have risen sharply and that companies are looking for more efficient wrapping methods (Inbound Logistics: https://www.inboundlogistics.com/articles/the-invisible-shortage-how-petrochemical-shortages-could-impact-packaging).

Logistics packaging is a classic second-order transmission channel. It is not the final product, but it enters the transport cost of almost every product. Likely effects include:

  • higher prices for stretch film, PP strapping, OPP tape, bubble wrap, and cushioning materials;
  • warehouses changing packing standards and reducing wrapping layers;
  • higher breakage rates and insurance/claims risk;
  • more pressure on small e-commerce sellers and small manufacturers that cannot easily absorb costs.

3. Daily necessities, paper goods, and sanitary products: not the paper itself, but resins, packaging, ink, and fuel

NHK WORLD reported that Daio Paper plans to raise prices by 15% or more for household and commercial products delivered from August, including tissue paper, toilet paper, kitchen paper, diapers, and sanitary products. The company cited rising costs for fuel, logistics, packaging materials, and ink (NHK WORLD: https://www3.nhk.or.jp/nhkworld/en/news/backstories/4799/). The same report notes that the Japanese government warned people not to panic-buy garbage bags because garbage bags also contain naphtha-derived materials.

In these categories, the most likely symptoms are not black-and-white packaging, but:

  • higher prices for tissue, toilet paper, kitchen paper, diapers, and sanitary products;
  • thinner outer packaging film or specification changes;
  • more promotion of large packs and refill formats;
  • panic-buying narratives or purchase limits for low-cost essentials such as garbage bags.

4. Paint, printing, and solvents: higher risk of volatile pricing and order restrictions

The "ink" in the food packaging story is only one corner of the broader ink and solvent system. Naphtha derivatives enter solvents, resins, coatings, thinners, adhesives, and printing inks. Tavily source discovery and multiple industry reports point to paint thinners, ink resins/solvents, and printing converter lead times and costs as potential bottlenecks.

Likely symptoms include:

  • printers restricting new orders or extending lead times;
  • shorter quote validity periods;
  • price increases in commercial printing, labels, and industrial markings beyond food packaging;
  • delays in automotive repair, building painting, and manufacturing coating work because solvent supply and prices are unstable.

5. Automobiles, electronics, and machinery: not a whole-car naphtha shortage, but small-part disruption risk

TDB's supply-chain analysis emphasizes that naphtha-derived products flow through basic chemicals and synthetic resins into final products such as electrical goods, automotive parts, clothing, and pharmaceuticals (TDB: https://www.tdb.co.jp/report/industry/20260417-naphtha26fy/).

For automobiles and electronics, the consumer will not usually see discolored packaging. The risk is more likely to be:

  • higher costs for wire coatings, connectors, seals, interior resin parts, and rubber parts;
  • a low-value small component slowing an entire machine or vehicle assembly process;
  • suppliers demanding price revisions, with automakers or electronics manufacturers pushing pressure downstream or reassigning suppliers;
  • slow substitution because alternative materials require certification cycles.

The logic resembles the auto chip shortage during the pandemic: not every chip was expensive, but a few critical components could halt the line. Under a naphtha shock, similar risks may appear in adhesives, seals, resins, films, insulation, and packaging components.

6. Healthcare materials: highest priority, hardest to substitute

Healthcare materials use large amounts of PP, PE, PVC, EVA, synthetic rubber, and sterile packaging materials. Disposable gloves, syringes, IV bags, tubes, dialysis circuits, pharmaceutical blister packs, and sterile packaging can all involve naphtha-derived materials.

There is now a clearer warning in healthcare. Reuters reported that if naphtha supply shortages worsen, some high-urgency medical devices related to dialysis and surgery could face shipment difficulties from around mid-April through August; Prime Minister Sanae Takaichi is believed to be aware of the risk, making the government response a key focus (Reuters: https://jp.reuters.com/markets/commodities/HJNOEZOE4RPQXNDYBVOA3CXZMA-2026-03-27/). Mechanically, healthcare materials have three high-risk features:

  • large volumes and high reliance on disposable items;
  • certification requirements for food/medical contact materials, making substitution slow;
  • the issue cannot be solved simply by "printing less," because the core problem is the material itself and sterile packaging.

Healthcare is therefore more likely to receive priority protection from the government than to be left to pure price pass-through. Future signals to watch include explicit priority allocation policies or hospital consumables price revision notices.

Why Companies Downgrade or Suspend Orders Even When Aggregate Supply Is "Enough"

The key distinction in this research is that upstream aggregate supply, downstream usability, and consumer-facing availability are three different things.

The government cares whether naphtha or naphtha-derived chemical products can support the overall national economy. Companies care whether a specific material can be delivered on a specific date, in a specific specification. There are multiple bottleneck layers in between:

  1. feedstock: whether naphtha, crude oil, or alternative sources arrive;
  2. cracker: whether ethylene/propylene and other basic chemical capacity operates normally;
  3. resin/solvent: whether PE, PP, PVC, PET, solvents, and resins are available;
  4. converter: whether film, containers, adhesives, inks, and coating processes have capacity;
  5. compliance: whether food-contact, medical, and construction certifications allow substitution;
  6. user: whether food, daily goods, and construction-material manufacturers can use new materials on existing lines.

If any of layers 3-6 become blocked, firms will simplify packaging, raise prices, restrict supply, or suspend orders even if aggregate national volume looks adequate.

Policy Response: From Macro Supply Assurance to Downstream Triage

Policy developments also support this interpretation. Japan's Ministry of Agriculture, Forestry and Fisheries has set up a Middle East situation response portal, publishing information and requests related to stable supply of petroleum-derived food containers, packaging materials, agricultural plastics, lubricants, and related goods (MAFF: https://www.maff.go.jp/chuto_josei.html). Asahi reported that MAFF is investigating distribution conditions for 57 items, including food trays, bento containers, egg cartons, vegetable packaging films, agricultural materials, and fishing gear. Of those, six items such as rice bags, expanded polystyrene, and agricultural mulch sheets were described as having secured supply prospects (Asahi: https://www.asahi.com/articles/ASV4X21WPV4XUTIL00MM.html).

This suggests the government response is shifting from "can naphtha volume be maintained?" to "which downstream essential goods and agriculture/food materials need priority clearing?" If medical consumables, food containers, garbage bags, agricultural films, and construction repair materials compete for constrained inputs, policy will likely create a de facto priority order: healthcare and daily necessities above promotional packaging, decorative printing, and lower-priority consumer goods.

Market structure will likely split in three ways:

  • large companies will find it easier to secure materials and converter capacity, while smaller firms are more likely to be squeezed out;
  • standardized packaging and fewer-SKU companies will be more resilient, while companies dependent on small-batch multi-design packaging will be more fragile;
  • upstream packaging, ink, film, and adhesive suppliers will gain short-term pricing power, but may also be asked by the government to cooperate on essential-goods supply.

Scenario Outlook

Base Case: Summer/autumn price hikes plus normalized packaging simplification

This is the most likely scenario. Naphtha imports and alternative sourcing gradually improve, but downstream inventories, contracts, and conversion capacity lag. Food and daily-goods prices continue rising in summer/autumn; simplified packaging lasts for months; companies protect core SKUs and reduce nonessential designs.

Leading indicators:

  • more food and daily-goods price revision announcements after July;
  • continued price hikes from packaging material, ink, and film suppliers;
  • more packaging carrying "resource-saving" or "simplified packaging" explanations;
  • fewer limited-edition and promotional packages.

Stress Case: Downstream specification bottlenecks cause broader suspensions

If Middle East tensions persist, alternative imports arrive slowly, or converter capacity is insufficient, the shock will move from price transmission to supply transmission. Some small and midsize food companies, daily-goods companies, and construction-material suppliers may face temporary suspensions, stopped orders, or longer lead times.

Leading indicators:

  • more company notices using terms such as 休売 (sales suspension), 受注停止 (order suspension), or 出荷制限 (shipment restriction);
  • retail purchase limits for garbage bags, diapers, or packaged foods;
  • packaging suppliers refusing new orders;
  • public government coordination of priority supply for essential packaging materials.

Transformation Case: Low-ink, transparent, generic packaging becomes long-term

If consumers accept simplified packaging, companies may reframe crisis measures as environmental or resource-saving moves and keep some of the designs. Over the long term, packaging may move toward standardized, low-color, low-graphic, generic base designs.

Leading indicators:

  • companies rebrand "temporary packaging" as environmental or resource-saving packaging;
  • new products launch directly with lower-print designs;
  • retailers accept more generic packaging and sticker-based labeling;
  • packaging design KPIs shift from shelf impact to material resilience and supply stability.

Reversal Case: Upstream supply recovers, but prices stay high

Even if Hormuz shipping improves, companies may use the event to renegotiate contracts, raise safety stocks, and embed previously hard-to-pass-through packaging and logistics costs into prices. Consumers may see color packaging return, but prices may not fall.

Leading indicators:

  • packaging returns to color, but retail prices do not decline;
  • earnings reports mention structurally higher materials costs;
  • packaging suppliers' margins improve;
  • private labels and large-pack formats accelerate.

Implications by Participant

Large food brands

They have stronger procurement power and are more likely to secure packaging materials first, but they will sacrifice new products and limited editions. The likely playbook is: protect core SKUs, simplify packaging, and raise prices in phases.

Small and midsize food manufacturers

They face the highest risk. They lack negotiating power and may struggle to absorb packaging cost increases. If a specific container or film becomes unavailable, they may have to suspend sales directly.

Packaging, ink, and film suppliers

Their short-term bargaining power rises, but they also face raw material procurement pressure. Suppliers able to provide alternative materials, low-ink designs, rapid redesign support, and certification assistance will benefit.

Retailers and convenience stores

They are squeezed by both shortages and price increases in the short term. In the medium term, they may allocate more shelf space to suppliers with stable delivery. Whether private labels benefit depends on whether their packaging supply chains are more standardized.

Consumers

Consumers will first see packaging become uglier or simpler, then see price increases, smaller sizes, and fewer low-price products. Natto, paper goods, garbage bags, lactic acid beverages, bread, and processed foods are likely to be more noticeable everyday categories.

Conclusion: A Stress Test of Materials Infrastructure

The significance of Calbee's black-and-white packaging is not that potato chip bags look worse. It is that the incident makes the materials infrastructure behind modern consumer goods visible. For many products, the bottleneck is not whether the contents can be produced. It is whether the producer can secure compliant packaging, adhesives, inks, films, coatings, containers, and transport packaging.

The next phase should be judged by three questions:

  1. How many firms move from "price increases" to "supply restrictions or sales suspensions"?
  2. How many temporary simplified packages become permanent?
